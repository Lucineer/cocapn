# Experiments — Context Weaver

This document outlines experimental approaches for evaluating and improving Context Weaver's cross-domain synthesis capabilities. Each experiment is designed to test a specific hypothesis about how cross-domain connections contribute to creative thinking and problem-solving.

---

## Experiment 1: Cross-Domain Connections and Creative Output

### Hypothesis

Users who receive cross-domain connections from Context Weaver produce more creative solutions to open-ended problems compared to users who receive domain-specific assistance only.

### Method

**Participants**: 30 participants, each with at least two distinct interest domains (self-reported).

**Conditions**:
- **Control**: Participants interact with a standard assistant (no cross-domain suggestions)
- **Treatment**: Participants interact with Context Weaver (active cross-domain suggestions)

**Task**: Each participant is given three open-ended problems from different domains:
1. A design problem (redesign a common object)
2. A process problem (improve an existing workflow)
3. A conceptual problem (explain a complex idea to a new audience)

**Measurements**:
- **Fluency**: Number of distinct ideas generated
- **Originality**: Statistical novelty of ideas (rated by blind judges)
- **Flexibility**: Number of different domains/categories represented in ideas
- **Elaboration**: Depth of development for each idea

**Duration**: 60-minute sessions, three sessions per participant over one week.

### Metrics

- Ideas per session (count)
- Originality rating (1-7 Likert scale, averaged across 3 blind judges)
- Domain diversity score (Shannon entropy of idea categories)
- User self-report of "aha" moments (count per session)

### Expected Results

The treatment group should show higher originality and flexibility scores, with the effect increasing over sessions as the knowledge graph grows and becomes more personalized.

## Experiment 2: Analogy Quality Evaluation

### Hypothesis

Structural analogies generated by Context Weaver are rated as more useful than surface-level analogies by domain experts.

### Method

**Participants**: 10 domain experts (2 each from biology, music, programming, architecture, and cooking).

**Procedure**:
1. For each expert's domain, generate 10 analogies: 5 structural (relational mapping) and 5 surface-level (feature matching)
2. Present analogies in random order without labeling which type they are
3. Experts rate each analogy on: usefulness (1-7), surprise (1-7), and actionability (1-7)
4. After rating, experts classify each analogy as structural or surface-level

**Measurements**:
- Usefulness rating by analogy type
- Surprise rating by analogy type
- Expert classification accuracy (can they tell the difference?)

### Metrics

- Mean usefulness score (structural vs. surface)
- Mean surprise score (structural vs. surface)
- Classification accuracy (% correctly identified)
- Correlation between structural depth score and usefulness rating

### Expected Results

Structural analogies should score significantly higher on usefulness and actionability. Surface-level analogies may score higher on initial surprise but lower on sustained value. The `analogyThreshold` parameter should be calibrated to match expert judgment.

## Experiment 3: Serendipity Engine Surprise Value

### Hypothesis

Serendipitous cross-domain suggestions from Context Weaver are perceived as surprising but eventually useful, with utility increasing as the knowledge graph grows.

### Method

**Participants**: 15 regular users of Context Weaver.

**Procedure**:
1. Users interact with Context Weaver for 30 days
2. Serendipitous suggestions are tagged in the interaction log
3. After each serendipitous suggestion, users rate: surprise (1-7), relevance (1-7), and intent to explore (1-7)
4. At 7-day intervals, users review all serendipitous suggestions received and rate: which ones they explored, which ones proved useful, and which ones they dismissed

**Measurements**:
- Surprise rating over time (does the engine keep being surprising?)
- Conversion rate (serendipitous suggestions that were explored)
- Utility rate (explored suggestions that proved useful)
- Surprise-utility correlation (are the most surprising ones also the most useful?)

### Metrics

- Weekly surprise mean (track over 4 weeks)
- Conversion rate: # explored / # suggested
- Utility rate: # useful / # explored
- Knowledge graph growth rate: new nodes and edges per week

### Expected Results

Surprise ratings should remain stable or increase (as the engine accesses more diverse domains). Conversion rate should increase over time as the knowledge graph better calibrates to the user's interests. The highest-utility connections should come from the most surprising suggestions, confirming the value of bisociation.

## Experiment 4: Knowledge Graph Growth and Utility Over Time

### Hypothesis

The cross-domain knowledge graph grows logarithmically (fast initial growth, slowing over time as connections saturate) and its utility to the user grows linearly (as existing connections are refined and strengthened).

### Method

**Participants**: 10 long-term users of Context Weaver (minimum 90 days).

**Procedure**:
1. Track knowledge graph metrics automatically: nodes, edges, cross-domain edges, average connection strength
2. Track user interaction metrics: cross-domain suggestions accepted, suggestions explored, insights attributed to cross-domain connections
3. Monthly retrospective: users review their knowledge graph and rate its overall utility (1-7)
4. Compare early graph structure (first 30 days) to late graph structure (days 60-90)

**Measurements**:
- Graph size: total nodes and edges over time
- Cross-domain density: ratio of cross-domain edges to total edges
- Connection strength distribution: how strengths evolve over time
- User utility rating: monthly self-report
- Insight attribution: user-reported insights linked to specific graph connections

### Metrics

- Graph growth rate: new nodes per week (fitted curve)
- Connection strength evolution: mean strength at 30, 60, 90 days
- Utility rating trend (linear regression over 3 months)
- Insight attribution count per month

### Expected Results

Graph growth should follow a logarithmic curve as the space of obvious connections is explored and the agent begins finding deeper, less obvious ones. Utility should grow more steadily as existing connections are refined, strengthened, and connected to each other. The graph should become increasingly unique to each user, reflecting their individual combination of interests.

## General Methodological Notes

### Controls

All experiments should control for:
- **User domain expertise**: More knowledgeable users may evaluate analogies differently
- **Session length**: Longer sessions may produce different creative patterns
- **Domain pair distance**: Some domain pairs are naturally more connected than others
- **Order effects**: Presenting suggestions in different orders may affect evaluation

### Ethical Considerations

- Users should be informed that their interactions are being studied
- Knowledge graph data belongs to the user and should not be shared without consent
- Serendipity injection should be transparent — users should know when suggestions are serendipitous vs. directly relevant

### Data Collection

All experiment data is stored in the user's private repo (in the brain), consistent with cocapn's privacy-by-design principle. Aggregate results can be computed locally and shared in anonymized form.

### Iteration

These experiments are designed to be run iteratively. Results from each experiment inform adjustments to the analogy engine, serendipity injector, and knowledge graph scoring algorithms. The experimental framework itself is version-controlled in the repo.
